This course will expose participants to several common treatment adherence barriers which must be overcome by people living with diabetes. These include barriers to medication therapy adherence and necessary lifestyle changes, the patient’s ability to adhere to their prescribed therapy, and their ability to access adequate support mechanisms.  

Let's begin with our learning objectives.

 

Learning Objectives

The Learning Objectives for this course are fairly straightforward. While we will assume you have some knowledge of diabetes and related therapies, we will go deeper in an effort to help bolster your understanding of the many potential barriers which stand in the way of 100% adherence to therapy with every diabetes patient.

 

The purpose of setting learning objectives at the beginning of the course is to set the framework and goals for the information that we will cover.

 

Once the course is completed you should be able to:

1. List four reasons clients are non-adherent

2. Describe the four step strategy for detecting non-adherence

3. Define the two main categories of adherence strategies

 

This presentation will concentrate on non-adherence in diabetes management in the general population. The course will not specifically concentrate on any subgroups, such as pediatric, teen, or others which may have additional concerns or needs.

 

Background

There are several types of diabetes, including: Type 1, Type 2, Gestational, and others.

Type 1 diabetes was previously referred to as insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus or juvenile-onset diabetes. This type of diabetes accounts for about 5-10% of diabetes cases in the United States and is usually diagnosed during childhood or young adulthood. Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune disease in which the body’s immune system mistakenly attacks and destroys pancreatic beta cells, which are responsible for producing insulin. While the exact causes are unknown, heredity may play a role. And it should be noted that this type of diabetes can occur as a result of pancreatic damage from alcohol, disease, or surgery. People with this type of diabetes take insulin daily to mimic natural insulin release.

Type 2 diabetes was previously referred to as non-insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus or adult-onset diabetes. However, younger people may be diagnosed with this type of diabetes, and some people with type 2 diabetes are treated with insulin. Between 90-95% of diabetes cases fall into this category. It can be progressive, starting with insulin resistance which means that the targeted body cells don’t use insulin properly. Eventually the pancreas may not be able to produce an adequate amount of insulin, called secondary beta cell failure.

Gestational diabetes is diagnosed during pregnancy and occurs more commonly in obesity, those with family histories, and people of African, Hispanic/Latino, and American Indian descent. Treatment is geared to control maternal blood glucose levels to reduce the risk for health complications for the infant. Progression to type 2 diabetes is common, with 5-10% of women with diabetes immediately after pregnancy and a 40-60% chance of developing diabetes within 5-10 years.

Other types of diabetes account for 1-5% of all diabetes and are generally related to genetic predisposition, surgery, medication interactions, infections, pancreatic disease, or other conditions that affect the pancreas.

We should take a moment to mention pre-diabetes, which is a condition where the blood sugar level may be higher than normal, but not high enough to qualify for a diagnosis of diabetes. This condition is estimated to occur in nearly 80 million people in the U.S. or more than three times the number of people living with diabetes. While pre-diabetes increases risks for developing type 2 diabetes and its associated health problems, it may be reversed through weight loss and increased physical activity.

 

According to the National Diabetes Fact Sheet, more than 30 million people in the United States have diabetes. This represents more than 9.4% of the total population. The number of adults diagnosed in the last 20 years has more than tripled. Nearly 19 million have been diagnosed, while about 7 million remain undiagnosed. An additional 84 million are likely to have pre-diabetes. About 1.5 million adults are diagnosed with diabetes annually.[1]

Diabetes is a significant contributor to mortality with more than 231,000 death certificates showing diabetes as an underlying or contributing cause in 2007, the most recent year for which these statistics were collected.

Higher rates of heart disease and stroke, high blood pressure, blindness, kidney disease, neuropathy, and amputation are seen in diabetes.

Health care costs for people with diagnosed diabetes were estimated to be 2.3 times higher than without diabetes. The total cost of diabetes care in 2017 was $327 billion, which included $237 billion for direct costs and $90 billion for indirect costs.[1]

Diabetes can be difficult to treat because it involves patient behavior and requires adherence to complicated regimens, often including behavior modification in order to improve diet and exercise. Patients have to actively manage their disease and the efforts to both equip patients to deal with each of the treatment recommendations, and then to have them follow-through with those therapies, can be both labor-intensive and difficult. In addition, the coordination of patient care is likely to require different specialists to support a variety of treatments related directly to diabetes and indirectly to diabetes-related conditions. To achieve treatment goals of blood glucose control, patients may require an intensive education and team approach.
