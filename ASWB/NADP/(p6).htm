Medication Adherence Research

 

•Approximately 50% adherent
–Annual cost for non-adherence $290 billion
–Annual cost per patient doubles
•Increased adherence to diabetes medications
–Reduces hospitalizations
–Reduces emergency room visits
–Offsets the increased cost of drugs for adherence

 

 

While there is a lot of misinformation and speculation about adherence and outcomes, good examples may be best seen in research regarding medication adherence. When medications are prescribed to assist in the control of blood sugar, there are a number of critical points where non-adherence can occur, starting with filling and refilling prescriptions, and taking medications incorrectly. And at first glance, these adherence issues are not easily reflected in diabetes cost statistics. As you can imagine, a patient who doesn’t refill their prescriptions may have a lower cost of care for that particular part of their disease management but, as we will see, may have significantly higher costs for the consequences of their medication non-adherence.

Research on medication adherence suggests that the adherence rate for patients with diabetes is around 50%.[7] Adherence studies suggest that there may be both health and cost benefits to improving adherence. Annual health care costs for a non-adherent patient may average twice those of a patient who is adherent to their prescribed medication regimens. With a starting level of $290 billion for problems associated with non-adherence, there are a lot of savings to be had.

 

Improvement in adherence comes with both costs and benefits.[8] In a study on adherence in patients on oral diabetes medications, which included nearly 57,000 individuals with type 2 diabetes, when adherence rates were raised from 50% to 100%, hospitalization rates dropped 23.3%, a significant reduction. Emergency room visits were also reduced, by 46.2%. This came at an increased cost for medications--from about $325 to $1105 per person, but resulted in an overall savings of $1.12-$1.14 for each dollar spent on these medications.

Another recent study reported in October 2011 suggested that there were significant differences between more than 11,000 adherent and more than 400 non-adherent patients for severity of illness, income level, depression, and other potential factors.[9] Costs were increased in the non-adherent patients by $15,231 per year compared to $13,848 for the adherent patients. For the non-adherent patients, costs were specifically higher for inpatient and outpatient care. While pharmacy benefits were higher in adherent patients, this was offset by the significantly higher costs of inpatient and outpatient care. It should be noted that when the 291 high-cost outlier patients were excluded from the evaluation, the costs were slightly higher for adherent patients, even though inpatient and outpatient care was less, due to the higher pharmacy benefit taken advantage of by adherent patients. In addition, this study suggested that the degree of adherence was related to hospitalization rates with more adherence which reflected fewer hospitalizations, when high-cost outliers were excluded.

When we revisit what can be done to improve adherence, we will see that efforts to reduce the complexity of medication regimens have shown some promise. In a meta-analysis of 17 studies, fixed dose combination therapies, for instance, showed significant improvements in adherence in addition to the associated lower direct medical costs (including medication costs), and improved patient satisfaction.[10]
