Defining Terms

Research suggests that by following diabetes care guidelines regarding appropriate management for diabetes, outcomes can improve. Patient care standards are generally listed with the caution that all strategies should be tailored to individual patient conditions, including any additional diagnoses and their capacity to adhere to recommended and prescribed treatments. While outcomes can be improved by following evidence-based guidelines, addressing patient adherence is a key feature of successful diabetes management. So let’s first agree upon a definition of adherence and other terms in order to discuss the strategies to support it.

Patients and facilities may see impact on:

 

-Adherence

-Non-adherence

-Compliance

-Maintenance

-Self-Management

 

 

A medically-oriented definition for adherence can be found in most medical dictionaries. The Farlex Medical Dictionary defines adherence as: “The extent to which the patient continues the agreed-upon mode of treatment under limited supervision when faced with conflicting demands, as distinguished from compliance or maintenance.”[2] Another definition from the same source is, “the active, voluntary, and collaborative involvement of the patient in a mutually acceptable course of behavior to produce a therapeutic result.”[2] This suggests the patient and their caregivers will take an active role in collaboration with their health care team. It requires motivation and self-regulation of the illness and treatment. It should be noted that this concept can be applied to the many dimensions of diabetes care and treatment because a patient may be able to achieve adherence to one aspect of their care, yet not be adherent to another.

Non-adherence characteristics include a delay in seeking medical care, a failure to keep appointments, and/or a failure to follow instructions for prescribed treatment strategies. As we will see, adherence is influenced by a number of things, which should be assessed and tailored for each individual in order to improve adherence. If we know what can be changed to improve adherence, we may be able to systematically address each of these problems with the patient and their caregivers.

 

 

We’re using the term adherence here, because the more familiar term “compliance” implies a slightly different meaning. The definition of compliance implies a more passive role for the patient in achieving results from a prescribed set of strategies, with the definition of, “The degree of constancy and accuracy with which a patient follows a prescribed regimen, as distinguished from adherence or maintenance.”[3] Non-compliant patients may be seen as unable to follow instructions, which may place them in a defensive rather than collaborative position in disease management.

Maintenance may be defined as, “The extent to which a patient continues good health practices without professional supervision, as distinguished from adherence or compliance.”[3]

The final definition to agree on is self-management. This capacity to take care of oneself is based on a partnership and collaboration between patients and/or their caregivers and health care providers where each has responsibility for management segments. This includes interventions, training and skills development, all aimed at enabling patients to better learn to effectively take care of themselves. By including self-management education for chronic conditions such as diabetes, improvements in symptom management have been shown, as well as improvements in the amount of exercise and social activity the patient experiences, as well as the amount and quality of communication they have with health care professionals.[4] Additional results suggest that building self-management skills can reduce hospitalization and outpatient visits, with a cost-to-savings ratio in one case of 1:4 (one to four).[4] The benefits of promoting self-management can improve adherence, and can persist for years.

  

 

If you care for older adults, asymptomatic bacteriuria (ASB) is a condition that you may frequently encounter. ASB is common in people over 65. The prevalence of bacteriuria in people residing in long term care without a catheter are 18-57% for women and 19-38% for men![8] A midstream urine specimen (2 consecutive specimens in women) with a single bacteria presence of more than 100,000 colony-forming units per milliliter can be diagnostic for ASB.

Often this problem is misdiagnosed as UTI or CAUTI and the patient is subjected to unnecessary treatment, additional costs and the development of multi-drug resistant organisms. It is considered unnecessary to monitor and treat this condition since the intervention does not prevent future SUTI and is generally not associated with renal damage.[9] Care should be taken with appropriate clinical assessment skills to obtain accurate information and to substantiate a correct diagnosis.

According to authors from the National Institutes of Health, “it is imperative to distinguish symptomatic UTI from asymptomatic bacteriuria,” and that treatment of ASB, “increases the rate of adverse drug effects from the use of antimicrobial medicines; increases the rate of recurrent infections with multi-drug resistant (MDR) bacteria; and doesn’t change survival, chronic genitourinary symptoms, or the rate of SUTI.” The Infectious Diseases Society of America (IDSA) recommends against treatment of ABS.
