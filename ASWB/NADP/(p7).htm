Reasons for Non-Adherence

 

Reasons that have been given for non-adherence include the complexity of the regimens and interventions themselves, any real or perceived side effects of the medications, and the cost of the medications including care or reimbursement issues.[11, 12]

•Side effects of treatment
•Cost/reimbursement
•Neurologic disorders
•Psychological factors

In evaluating the level of adherence to different components of disease management, diet and physical activities were identified as the “least adhered to” by diabetes patients. Patients identified specific problems in adhering to dietary management, including figuring out how to dine out and how to handle other people offering inappropriate food choices. For physical exercise, patients identified negative physical reactions as the reason for non-adherence.[13]

Patients who experience negative side effects, or think they have, and those who may experience side effects may discontinue the use of a prescribed medication. Side effects are sometimes hard to isolate and require the team to work with the patient to find reasons for the side effects, such as interactions with other agents or the way the medication is being taken, in order to find acceptable solutions.

 

Costs for medications can be a significant barrier for the self-management of diabetes. Patients with diabetes may be faced with costs for diabetes-specific medications as well as medications for related complications, such as hypertension, cardiovascular disease, and others. It is fairly common for patients not to fill or not to refill their medication prescriptions, with cost being the primary reason given for about 40% of the respondents in one survey.[14] An estimated 36 million Americans could not afford prescription medications, according to a report from the Center for Studying Health System Change. And, there were about 28.5 million uninsured Americans, about 8.8% of the population, according to the Census Bureau survey from 2017.[15]

Specific to diabetes, persons who are older, under or un-insured, elderly with depression, or low-income African Americans and Latino Americans noted their reason for medication non-adherence to be based on cost.

Typical coping strategies include forgoing medications and skipping doses. Alternately, some patients have reported forgoing food or other essentials, or borrowing on credit cards or from family and friends, in order to maintain medication regimens. Often, patients may not communicate these issues to health care providers because of a lack of time during visits, concern over the discomfort the topic may cause, and/or a lack of habit in discussing finances with strangers.

 

There are other variables to consider, including the presence of neurological disorders (such as Alzheimer’s disease and dementia). And there are psychological factors such as depression, which can also contribute to non-adherence.

In some cases, the relationship between the health care provider and patient can be strained by differences in culture, communication, and misunderstandings. In some cases, patients may not ask questions or voice concerns if they are intimidated by their health care providers--for any number of reasons. One study that evaluated reasons for non-adherence from a physician point-of-view suggested frustration with patient adherence based on a lack of patient understanding, including knowledge, beliefs, and attitudes about diabetes, the importance of medications, and the influence of friends as a priority over medical professional advice.[16] Practitioners recognize that their efforts don’t always meet patient expectations, and practitioner frustration can lead to paternalistic attitudes and communication styles. In some cases, practitioners may assume that the best way to motivate patients is by applying pressure and using scare tactics. Overall, practitioners suggested that a structured consultation and continued follow-up with a multidisciplinary team could assist in building adherence, and cautioned that there is not one single type of diabetes patient, which means that all efforts have to be carefully tailored to meet the individual needs of the patient.

Other reported reasons for non-adherence include missed appointments and a lack of understanding of the illness.
