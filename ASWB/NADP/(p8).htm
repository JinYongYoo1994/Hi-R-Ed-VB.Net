Detecting Non-Adherence

 

When treatment goals are not being achieved, it is important to consider non-adherence, and strategies to improve adherence.

 

•Objective and subjective evaluations
•4-Step strategy
–Observe patient for

      signs of adherence

–Use a structured

      interview

–Share the decision-

      making with the patient

–Ask about adherence

 

First, patients can be directly observed for signs of adherence, and measurements of biological markers (glucose and hemoglobin A1c), along with the concentration of medications in the blood can be used as direct indicators. Other efforts may include patient diaries, measurement of related markers (such as blood pressure), refill rates, and pill counts. For medications, there is an expectation of a percentage that hemoglobin A1c can be lowered. For instance, the use of metformin, an oral medication, may decrease A1c by 1.5%. If the medication doesn’t appear to work, non-adherence should be explored.

It has been suggested that clinicians may overestimate adherence, or on the other hand, may not detect non-adherence. It may be difficult to detect non-adherence through patient reports. Inaccurate information may be given for a number of reasons, including patient perception, or concern with the repercussions of “admitting” non-adherence.[17] Inaccurate information can lead to changes in therapies which can cause treatments to become ineffective, or unnecessary treatments, which can cause additional side effects and costs. Some methods may include a combination of subjective and objective measures, including laboratory values, pill counts, food diaries, other monitoring logs, and patient reports. Describing some of the risk factors to target patients who may have non-adherence issues can also help. These indicators include missing appointments, non-responsiveness to treatment, and patient self-reports.[17]

 

Second, structured questionnaires or patient interviews conducted by a skilled interviewer may be a practical tool for identifying non-adherence.[18] Asking open-ended questions that cannot be answered with a simple “yes” or “no” should be used, and yet be specific enough to allow the clinician to learn about the patient’s understanding. For instance, rather than asking if a patient has had any problems, the clinician can ask, “How do you take your medications?” This can allow the patient to respond with their understanding of how medications can be taken – with or without food, before or after eating, doses, frequencies, and other items of interest. Continued probing can allow a better understanding of whether a prescription has been refilled, or something else that may affect adherence.

By normalizing, or universalizing non-adherence, barriers to accurate reporting can be reduced.[19] This can reverse the patient’s fear of a judgmental response by the health care provider. Statements such as, “everyone has some problems with adherence”, including specific examples (like time constraints, costs, or the complexity of their medication regimens), will help to acknowledge that there can be difficulties in adhering to prescribed therapies.

 

Third, be sure to create a shared decision-making process. This can be accomplished by explaining how treatment steps and decisions are based on the patient’s report, and that the accuracy of the information collected will help to best define the next steps. Discussing the problem with the patient by explaining that the patient and care provider each have an active role in the solution, will help to define the importance of patient participation and accuracy. For instance, telling the patient, “Your lab results from last week were a little high. We want to make sure that you are getting enough medication to control your blood sugar, but also want to be careful so as not to over treat you. To do this, we need to discuss reasons why the results might have been high this last time.”

The final and last step is to inquire about adherence. This allows the patient to understand the value of accurately reporting adherence without cornering them or forcing them to recant an earlier statement. It is helpful to ask about both recent and previous adherence behavior to learn about any changes in behaviors, or new barriers.
