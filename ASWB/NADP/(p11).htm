Self-Monitoring

 

SMBG is one of the methods which allows patients to take control of their therapies and learn what works best for them. This method has been used for about 30 years, and recent improvements have made it easier and more palatable for patients to achieve. However, research suggests that this method of monitoring is not well-adhered to, with about 24% of insulin-treated patients, 65% of patients on oral medications, and 80% of patients on diet and exercise alone not routinely performing SMBG, with some never doing it.[30] Not adhering to the prescribed frequency of SMBG can limit the effectiveness of self-management and efforts to build adherence behaviors.

The other question is about how well SMBG results reflect the hemoglobin A1c results in the same patient. The Fremantle Diabetes Study evaluated this question and suggested that SMBG results reflect prevailing blood glucose.[30] While the pre-meal and post-meal results are correlated, some refinements in interpretation and application may be required to improve its effectiveness in use. SMBG may vary in usefulness according to the patient population using it. A review of evidence for SMBG in patients with type 2 diabetes who do not use insulin suggested that there may not be significant clinical value, thereby reducing the potential for cost effectiveness.[30]

Some questions remain as to which patients will benefit most from SMBG and the cost-effectiveness for different sub-categories of diabetes care and treatment.[31] There are even SMBG “apps” available now for the iPhone!

•Real-time continuous glucose monitoring (RT-CGM)

•Comparative studies
 

 

Recent studies have compared real-time continuous glucose monitoring, or CGM, with SMBG on an intermittent basis.[32] CGM has been calibrated with SMBG results, making it easier and more realistic for comparisons to be made. In one study, at the end of a 3-month intervention, hemoglobin A1c values were decreased and sustained in the follow-up 40-week period.[33] A meta-analysis of 19 trials suggested that CGM-users showed a significant reduction in hemoglobin A1c values, but that they were not significantly different from the results of studies that evaluated the use of SMBG.[34]

 

Community Resources

 

There are a number of community resources which can help both the clinician and the patient with diabetes when trying to improve adherence and self-management. Local, hospital-based programs exist which can help to raise awareness of the disease, as well as support groups which help to support the patient’s self-management and adherence to medications. These programs roughly fall in three categories:

1. Awareness campaigns

2. Support groups for patients and their caregivers

3. Online support

 

-Awareness campaigns aim to increase the general public’s, or a specifically targeted high-risk group’s, general level of education regarding diabetes. Specifically, what it is, why it’s serious, and how to prevent it. Examples include television campaigns, organized charity walks and fun runs.

-Support groups assume a higher level of understanding about the disease and so generally try to reinforce the importance of self-management and adherence to therapy amongst their members. They do this principally through group meetings and activities, and are found at the local level in many communities, and through the local hospital or clinic.

-Online support typically consists of support groups who meet online, and web sites created by people with diabetes for others with diabetes to use as a resource. These can provide personal information about the individual’s experiences with diabetes, as well as various resources for help at all stages of the disease.

The American Diabetes Association lists several types of community resources and links to program descriptions on their website, which are targeted to different high-risk populations. Target groups include African Americans, Native Americans, Latinos, as well as general efforts to spread the word on diabetes. The ADA also has several employer-based programs designed to impact higher risk populations by raising awareness at work.

The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention lists projects that address diabetes. These links may be most useful for clinicians looking for more information on activities which support the fight against diabetes, as well as new research and treatment trends.

A number of web sites can be found which have information about the disease, as well as links to local support groups. And most hospitals have diabetes support groups, and many of these groups have mailing lists with regular releases of information pertinent to the disease and its management. These groups are often moderated by a Certified Diabetic Educator or other qualified professional, and can supply help and support to both patients and their caregivers.

 

Summary

 

Even the best-evidenced guidelines require follow-through. And, with diabetes, follow-through by the patient and their caregivers is of utmost importance in order to achieve therapy goals. The complexity and behavior modifying strategies that are typically required mean that they will be essential to both achieve patient buy-in, and building the patient’s for self-management.

It is an ongoing process to evaluate, educate, and monitor for the need to adjust strategies, as well as to monitor for changing patient needs with regard to their old issues and new ones. All of these can challenge adherence and treatment effectiveness.

As we have seen, therapies can work, patient adherence to therapies can improve outcomes, and both quality and cost-effective health care depends on the ability for health care providers and patients to better understand the barriers to adherence and the ways to overcome them. These benefits include decreases in the number, and severity of, adverse events related to the disease and its management, as well as to an improvement in overall health, quality of life, and patient satisfaction.
