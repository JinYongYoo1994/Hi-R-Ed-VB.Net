Traveling

There are some basic components to these CGMs. It’s important to know what they are as it helps to understand how they work.

•Sensor
•Transmitter
•Receiver
–Dexcom – stand-alone
–Guardian – stand-alone
–Revel – combined insulin

      pump and CGM receiver

–iPro – integrated data

      storage device

- The sensor – consisting of a small hook with a thin metal wire.

- The transmitter – a small device, about the size of a 25 cent quarter, which sends the glucose reading to the receiver wirelessly.

- Receiver – a device, the size of a pager or cellphone, which receives and stores glucose readings. It can be placed on a table, kept in a bag or pocket or clipped to a belt. For Dexcom, it can receive the data up to 20 feet away, and is a stand-alone CGM receiver. The Medtronic unit can receive data from up to 6 feet away. There are three Medtronic systems: the Guardian, a stand-alone CGM receiver, the Revel, which is a combined insulin pump and CGM receiver, and the iPro, a blinded, stand-alone small CGM device which is attached to the body. An iPro stores glucose readings in itself for upload later into its CGM software. It looks identical to the Medtronic transmitter and is used on patients for PCPs to do the placement and interpretations. All three Medtronic CGM systems are approved by the FDA to be worn for three days, while the Dexcom is approved to be worn for 7 days. All three Medtronic systems use the same Medtronic Sof-Sensor, while the Dexcom uses the Dexcom Sensor.

 

Lagging and Leading Trends

•Testing for blood glucose – essential
•Glucose  - Blood glucose
  - Interstitial glucose
•Lagging and leading

12:30 PM blood glucose starts rising

12:50 PM interstitial glucose starts rising (lagging information)

2:00 PM blood glucose peaks

2:10 PM interstitial glucose peaks (lagging information)

3 PM interstitial glucose starts dropping (leading information)

3:05 PM blood glucose starts dropping

•Treatment decisions based on blood glucose, not interstitial glucose

Testing for blood glucose is necessary to calibrate and verify the glucose level since CGMs looks at the interstitial glucose level, while testing for blood glucose looks at the capillary glucose level. The latter is at the functional cellular stage where biochemical exchanges take place. The former reflects what was at the cellular level 5 to 20 minutes earlier, thus the term ‘lagging’. Interstitial glucose also reflects what will be at the cellular level 5 to 20 minutes later, a ‘leading’ trend. As an example, let’s look at how this relates to a lunch eaten at 12 PM:

At 12:30 PM blood glucose starts rising

At 12:50 PM interstitial glucose starts rising (lagging information)

At 2:00 PM blood glucose peaks

At 2:10 PM interstitial glucose peaks (lagging information)

Then at 3 PM interstitial glucose starts dropping (leading information)

And at 3:05 PM blood glucose starts dropping.

It is important to note that the lagging and leading times are not consistent, ranging from 5 to 20 minutes on average. Moreover, the above is a simplistic look to illustrate this concept. Physiologically, multiple variables are in play at any given time, so glucose levels will be modified not only by food, its amount, state and composition, but also by the body’s metabolic rate, recent physical activity, response to any types of stress, the initial blood glucose level and the body’s response to it, certain medications, and so on. Interstitial glucose provides a trend, and blood glucose provides the actual glucose level at that moment in the body. Always follow the blood glucose result for any treatment decision.
