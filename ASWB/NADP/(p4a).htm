Overview of Treatment Strategies

Let's take a minute to talk about devices. In terms of diabetes self management, this refers to insulin pumps, pens and syringes and continuous glucose monitors.

Insulin syringes have varying needle length and are generally quite thin and virtually painless. Insulin pens are considered faster and easier than syringes, and are convenient, provide accurate dosing, and use very thin disposable needles.

Insulin is delivered subcutaneously using a variety of injection sites, which may include the abdomen, sides (love handles), thighs, buttocks, and arms. It is suggested that a variety of spots should be used at each site. Pinching and cleansing is not generally necessary. A few seconds should be allowed for full delivery.

Insulin pumps are external, but connected to an indwelling subcutaneous catheter to provide rapid-acting insulin at programmable basal rates. It eliminates extra injections, has a dose calculator function, and provides a bolus history. Pumps require the support of qualified professionals.

There are also injection ports that can allow a single injection site to be used for several days before replacement.

For non-insulin using type 2 diabetes, the patient may not need to monitor blood sugars quite as closely once their routine for diet, exercise, and medications are set up and comfortable. Glucose monitoring can be rotated and conducted when there is a change in diet and exercise routine.

Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) systems work using a sensor under the skin to test glucose levels. These devices are approved by the FDA and are available by prescription. They allow the person with diabetes to better control over their blood sugar levels by allowing them to see detailed trending data, as collected by the continuous glucose monitor. However, it should be noted that users will still need to confirm glucose levels with a glucose meter before making changes in their treatments.
