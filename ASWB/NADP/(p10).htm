Building Adherence Behavior

 

In order to gain the patient’s trust, health care providers may need to take steps to establish a good rapport.

Involving the family in the support function serves a number of purposes, including reducing the patient’s and caregiver’s feelings of being overwhelmed, plus it reinforces the treatment strategies outlined, and allows for more understanding about the stressors in the patient’s life that may cause non-adherence. Perceptions affecting self-regulation can be affected by a family history of diabetes, and have been associated with treatment control, the level of understanding of their illness, and their dietary adherence and how well they monitor their own adherence.[24]

Home situations that may create barriers to self-management should be assessed. These may include family and caregiver behaviors that adversely affect efforts to integrate self-management activities into daily routines, as well as affecting the physical home environment. For instance, the availability of refrigeration, telephone service, cooking facilities, and sanitation should impact the decisions regarding the management strategies that can be accomplished by the patient in their home.

Cultural and other psychosocial factors can affect adherence behavior. In one report of a culturally-sensitive peer support program developed to target older Mexican-Americans in El Paso, Texas, occupational therapists trained peer leaders to lead and manage a community-based diabetes self-management program. Results from this small study of 42 Mexican-American elders with type 2 diabetes showed significant improvements from baseline with regard to blood sugar levels and self-efficacy. A predominant theme of the program included “choice + control = power”.[25]

Establishing routines and inserting interventions into a patient’s daily schedule can help to improve adherence, especially when considering medication regimens. The patient not only requires information about medications, but benefits from experience with both the medication and potential complications, the social support, and establishing useful routines.[26]

Additional evaluation and care referrals can be made for identified barriers and challenges. Or, gaps in information can be shored up by diabetic educators and socioeconomic concerns may benefit from a social work referral.

More intense monitoring and education efforts may require the addition of home care visits, especially for high risk patients and those with advanced conditions that make self-care more difficult to achieve.

 

Supporting Adherence

Using an approach that suggests that only the health care provider is the expert and the one who really knows what is best for the patient, and that they will advise the patient on what they should do can, in reality, disregard what may really be best for the patient, ignoring what the patient truly can accomplish, and forgo the patient’s autonomy to accomplish self-management.[27]

An example of a program to promote adherence and self-management in diabetes can be seen in the Stanford School of Medicine’s Diabetes Self-Management Program.[28] This program is six weeks in length, with six weekly classes for groups of 12-16 patients with type 2 diabetes. The topics include:

1) techniques to deal with the symptoms of diabetes,

2) exercise to improve their strength and endurance,

3) healthy eating strategies,

4) appropriate use of medications, and

5) working effectively with health care providers.

The program requires active participation with weekly action plans, sharing experiences, and helping each other to solve the problems they identify.

Another program tested the differences between interventions that included continuous glucose monitoring and either a problem-solving or more general diabetes education program in women with a history of suboptimal blood glucose control. The problem-solving intervention introduced barriers between the researchers and their subjects, which contrasted with the general education program that fostered a helpful, “we’re all on the same team” approach. The education program-centered approach was more effective, with the most significant results having been seen in the development of problem solving skills, as well as a trend toward better dietary adherence, increased exercise, weight loss, and satisfaction with their care.[29]
