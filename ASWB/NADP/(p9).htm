Improving adherence requires that patients and their caregivers have adequate information about diabetes, diabetes management, and their own specific circumstances that affect diabetes management decisions.

Patient-centered education, counseling, and problem-solving skill-building activities are often a part of formalized programs to assist in improving self-management actions. This may require close monitoring, reinforcement, and support by health care team members in collaboration with the patient and their family support network.

In some cases, the simplification of medications and other treatment strategies which have been tailored and simplified for the patient can help to remove complexity. For instance, combination medications may reduce the number of doses required during a day. A simplified meal and exercise plan may get the patient on the road to better results, and motivate them to achieve more as their understanding and good experiences improve.

 

When dealing with medication costs, it will first be important for patients to understand the importance of their prescribed medications in achieving disease management, and the value in doing so. The health care team can work with their patient to find acceptable cost-cutting solutions. These can include choosing medications that can achieve lower costs, such as generic drugs, or lowering the number of prescriptions through the use of combination therapies, over-the-counter substitutions, and therapeutic alternatives. Another avenue is to explore supportive resources such as patient assistance programs, which can be shared with patients and their care providers.

Additional efforts to improve outcomes will include giving support and reinforcement for the patient’s efforts to change their lifestyle in order to decrease medication complications, and the resultant higher costs of care. These include healthful eating choices, physical activity, and smoking cessation. Patients can be made aware that by implementing less costly healthy lifestyle choices, they may be able to lower the costs of care through the decreased use of health care services and prescription medications.

 

Now that we have reviewed some ways to improve adherence, we can discuss the specific agents for change. We can put adherence strategies into two main categories, physical agents and supportive activities.

Physical agents can be things like forms and pill boxes. Some specific items that have been employed include dosage forms, medication calendars, reminder charts, memory devices (such as pagers or watches), medication containers, special caps and systems, and specific labeling and packaging. Supportive actions may include education, setting reminders and periodic check-ins. Routine visits to reinforce, shape, and otherwise modify behaviors, programs that allow periodic check-ins with health care providers and educators, and support groups all fit within this category. Some behavior modification techniques may vary according to the patient. These can include prompting with physical, verbal, or other cues, or token economies, which include the use of reinforcing tokens that can be exchanged for items of value to the patient (materials, services, or privileges).

Let’s spend a few more moments on patient-centered care. Starting with treatment goals, keep in mind that these can be individualized, but generally include the control of blood glucose, which is monitored through intermittent measurements of blood glucose and hemoglobin A1c (a longer-term indicator).

Selected patient-centered care and patient responsibilities are listed here. Fairly strong evidence supports the inclusion of diabetes self-management education (or DSME), which is considered essential to the success of diabetes care.[20] DSME is an ongoing process to support the patient in building the knowledge and skills necessary to optimally manage their diabetes. In addition to clinical skills and knowledge, DSME can address psychosocial issues that are associated with good outcomes. Expert opinion and consensus suggests ongoing monitoring for psychosocial issues that can impair the patient’s ability to adhere to therapy recommendations.[21]

 

There is strong evidence to support Medical Nutrition Therapy, or MNT, that is individualized to support the patient’s appropriate dietary intake. Because one meal plan does not fit all diabetes patients, referral to a registered dietitian has routinely become a part of the recommendation to assure that the best mix of nutrients can be accomplished by the patient on a daily basis. Because there is good evidence to support the resultant health care cost savings when MNT is employed, it is covered by Medicare.

Lifestyle interventions for type 2 diabetes have shown sustained results, including weight loss, reduction in hemoglobin A1c, and reduced risk factors for cardiovascular diseases.[22] Very good evidence supports the encouragement of patients to exercise, and to include both aerobic and resistance exercise.[23]

There are additional goals for diabetes-related conditions, such as cardiovascular disease, retinopathy, nephropathy and high blood pressure, and neuropathy, most specifically in the feet. Team management with specialists in these areas includes routine examinations, and often include additional therapies to diabetes-specific management. All of these can increase the complexity of care, and challenge the patient’s adherence efforts.
