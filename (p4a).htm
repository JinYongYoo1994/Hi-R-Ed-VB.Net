Overview of Treatment Strategies

First, let’s overview the treatment strategies for type 1 and type 2 diabetes. We will need to keep in mind that there are several guidelines, and that all guidelines for care need to be individualized to the patient’s specific situation and other conditions which may affect their disease and its treatment.

In type 1 diabetes, there is a diminished ability to synthesize or secrete insulin. Insulin replacement is the key strategy, which can be done through an insulin injection or insulin pumps. Insulin pumps include tubed, tubeless, and implanted types. Diet and exercise education are also important supportive strategies in type 1 diabetes.

There are four mutually exclusive categories of insulin that can be used.[5] Within each of the categories, there is more than one option available, which affects the onset, peak and duration of action. These categories include rapid acting (which has an effect within about 15 minutes and a duration of about 1-5 hours), short acting (which has an effect in 30-60 minutes and lasts 2-8 hours), intermediate acting (with an onset of action in about 1-2 hours, peak effects between 4-12 hours, and duration of 18-24 hours), and long acting (which has an effect in 30 minutes to 2 hours and lasts 20-36 hours). The insulin categories are usually combined to offer both shorter and longer coverage. There are also premixed insulins, which are a combination of short and intermediate acting insulin. You can see that a prescription of insulin can be complicated, requiring the planning of mealtimes and medication administration schedules, which can be daunting to many patients. The premixed insulins can certainly reduce the complexity of the regimen, and we will see why that is important to adherence. Self-monitoring of blood glucose is strongly recommended to assist and support both maintenance and self-management activities.

In type 2 diabetes, strategies may range from efforts to sensitize cells to endogenous insulin and eventually, as the pancreas loses its ability to synthesize and secrete insulin, to replace the deficit of insulin. Education on both diet and exercise to control blood sugar and improve risk factors, such as obesity, are first line treatments. Diet education may include learning about the effect of carbohydrates on blood sugar, and how to “count carbs” in order to “even out” and reduce the effect on blood sugar levels.[6] Beyond carbohydrates, diabetes-related conditions may require additional dietary education to learn more about reducing risk factors for complications through the modulation of fiber, fat, and sodium. In addition to diet, exercise is an important part of health maintenance and can be tailored to reduce the risk for diabetes-related conditions, including cardiovascular disease and hypertension. Lifestyle changes are difficult for many patients to achieve, and recommendations by health care providers should be carefully tailored and monitored while the patient works toward their self-management goals.

Insulin sensitizing medications, such as metformin, may also be used to improve blood sugar control. Research areas for blood sugar control include incretins, which have a gastrointestinal effect to decrease carbohydrate absorption, improve the feeling of fullness after eating, and increase the amount of insulin that the pancreas secretes. Medications using this strategy block the degradation of these gastrointestinal hormones. Another fairly new approach is to stimulate the kidneys to eliminate more blood sugar.

SMBG has been the topic of much research. Recent guidelines suggest strong evidence for the use of SMBG in patients on multiple insulin injections or insulin pump therapies. For less frequent or non-insulin therapies, SMBG has been categorized as useful in determining the success of therapy, based on expert opinion or consensus.
